  • "The participant imaging centres will lead calculations of absorbed doses and related uncertainties. Protocols will be developed for each stage of image processing and dosimetry calculations and applied to the data collected. All anonymised raw and reconstructed patient image data from each imaging centre will be uploaded to each dosimetry site for processing. Tomographic image data will be reconstructed and corrected for scatter and attenuation. Image registration will be performed on all sequential image data with the CT acquired during hybrid imaging. Volumes of uptake will be identified and delineated. Normal organs will be identified and the absorbed doses calculated. In addition to mean dosimetry, 3D dosimetry will be performed for organs of uptake enabling production of dose volume histograms. Uncertainty analysis will be performed according to newly developed EANM guidelines [32]. An inter-comparison of dose estimates obtained from all imaging centres will be made to identify discrepancies and to finalise and optimise a dose reconstruction protocol that is reproducible and applicable in clinical practice. A common software for the 3D dosimetry, incorporating uncertainty analysis, will be agreed upon. Recommendations will be produced in conjunction with WP6 to use this protocol and software for accurate patient-specific dosimetry. Results will be fed at an early stage into Tasks 3.2 and 3.5 for initial development of a comprehensive biokinetic model and the epidemiological study and will continue to inform development as further data become available and are analysed."

'has description'

  • "This Subtask aims to estimate organ doses from the two most commonly used PET and SPECT tracers identified in Subtask 2.3.1. This will involve the conduct of a prospective study on consenting patients to acquire the necessary data to estimate the absorbed organ doses they received from these tracers while undergoing diagnostic scans in nuclear medicine. Protocol development, ethics submission and request for regulatory approval will commence following successful evaluation of this proposal. Research sites will be required to have obtained ethical and management approval and undertake a site initiation meeting prior to opening to recruitment. Once these have been obtained, recruitment of patients will commence among patients referred to nuclear medicine in routine clinical practice. Potential study subjects will be approached, informed about the study and asked whether they wish to participate in the study. Those who accept will be asked to sign an informed consent form. The aim is to recruit at least 25 participants. The study will not affect patient management. As this study concerns routine diagnostic scans in nuclear medicine, no problems with patient recruitment are expected. Apart from the standard diagnostic PET/CT or SPECT/CT scan they have been referred for, eligible patients will undergo 4 additional quantitative PET or SPECT scans at different time points, covering the physical and physiological decay of the tracer. Scans will take from between 20–40 minutes, dependent on the level of activity remaining. Further radiopharmaceutical administrations will not be necessary. Data will be captured, anonymized and transferred to INSERM and UGENT for dosimetry calculations. Organ dose calculation will be performed using Monte-Carlo simulation techniques in models for the 25 patients, with a specific focus on the cardiac and chest region. Updated administered activity-to-dose conversion factors will be produced and compared with the currently used ICRP conversion factors. Relevant guidance documents for this clinical study include: ICRP Publication 128 and Lassmann et al 201123,24."

  • "Estimation of patient organ doses from two commonly used PET and SPECT tracers"

'has description'

  • "CT scanning is used in multi-modality imaging for attenuation correction, anatomic localization and/or acquisition of additional diagnostic information. A method for organ and tissue doses estimation from diagnostic chest CT, also applicable to CT units of multi-modality systems, will be developed in Subtask 2.1.2. Using the same MC platform, the current subtask aims for a patientspecific organ dose estimation from a) cone-beam CT scanners used in multi-modality systems and b) CT scanners dedicated to attenuation correction and anatomic localization. This will focus on the most frequently used chest CT protocols and those with the highest contribution to the collective dose to patients in PET/CT and SPECT/CT identified in Subtask 2.3.1. A retrospective study will be conducted based on chest CT examinations performed on at least 200 adults. As in 2.1.2, using anonymised patient CT images as input volume, 3D patient models will be created for each patient. 3D dose distributions will be generated for the most important CT protocols used in PET/CT and SPECT/CT. MC simulations will be performed using an equipment-specific and patient-specific Monte Carlo code (ImpactMC). At least 5 modern CT scanners will be modelled. The scanners' geometry, X-ray spectrum, composition and dimensions of the filters will be taken into account for the simulation. Normalized to CTDIvol organ absorbed doses will be determined for all radiosensitive organs within the primary exposed volume. The latter data will serve as a basis for patient-specific organ doses estimates, taking into account patients’ body size. The resulting dose estimations will be essential novel information for dose optimisation of chest PET/CT and SPECT/CT procedures in 2.3.2.3, benefit-to-risk evaluation, epidemiological research and for modelling of radiation-induced cancer risk."

  • "Estimation of patient organ doses from chest CT used in multi-modality systems"

'has description'

  • "Chest CT examinations performed at UoC on at least 90 paediatric and 200 adult patients with well-defined clinical indications will be included in this study. Using patient CT images as input volume, a 3D model will be created for each patient. 3D dosedistributions will be generated for each, using an equipment-specific and patient-specific Monte Carlo code. Two sets of simulations will be performed for each patient using: i) a fixed tube current value and ii) tube current modulation. Normalized to CTDIvol organ absorbed dose will be determined for all radiosensitive organs located within the primary exposed volume i.e. breast, oesophagus, lungs, bone marrow, heart. At least 3 state-of-the-art CT scanners will be modelled. The scanners’ geometry, x-ray spectrum, composition and dimensions of the filters will be taken into account in the simulation. The number of photons produced for each simulation is estimated to be 108-109, resulting in a statistical inaccuracy lower than 1%. Radiation doses thus estimated will be compared to those determined using thermoluminescence dosimetry in a family of anthropomorphic phantoms (4 paediatric phantoms of different sizes and an adult phantom), software packages and data from the literature. Percentage differences in organ radiation doses will be presented and discussed. A patient-specific method for the estimation of organ doses will be finalised based on Monte Carlo simulation and intercomparison results, taking into accountpatients’ body size. This will be useful for CT dose optimisation and as input for epidemiological research and modelling of radiation-induced risks. Organ doses from CTs will be used together with age, sex and organ specific risk models proposed by BEIR VII9 and/or more recent appropriate risk models to derive lifetime attributable risks (and uncertainties) of cancer associated with chest CT scans building on existing risk projection tools [17,19,20]."
